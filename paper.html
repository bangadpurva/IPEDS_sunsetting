The Core Question

Are U.S. academic programs trending in the same direction as occupational demand — and critically, at the right credential level?

Most prior research answers one side of this. We answered both simultaneously and linked them through a single reproducible pipeline using only federal public data.

Three Data Sources, One Pipeline

IPEDS Completions Survey — what programs are producing graduates and how is that changing year over year.

BLS Employment Projections (2024-2034) — where occupational demand is headed over the next decade.

BLS OEWS National Files (2019-2024) — historical employment levels enabling lag analysis.

The Linking Layer: CIP-to-SOC Crosswalk

Academic programs are classified by CIP codes. Occupations are classified by SOC codes. NCES maintains a many-to-many crosswalk between them. We used this to map 35 CIP2 fields to 740+ unique SOC occupations, enabling direct comparison of supply trends to demand signals at the field and credential level.

01
Data Ingestion

Load and Filter IPEDS Files

Six annual CSV files (c2019_a through c2024_a) are loaded. Records are filtered to primary majors only (MAJORNUM = 1) and sub-associate award levels (AWLEVEL 1-2) are removed. The completion count column CTOTALT is extracted.

Each row is aggregated by CIP2 (first two digits of the 6-digit CIP code) and AWLEVEL per year, giving annual program-level headcounts.

Statistical Methods
What each method is, why it was chosen, and the key values derived
Method 01

Weighted Winsorization

What: Clips extreme net percentage change values to a bounded range before computing distributional thresholds, then weights the distribution by program baseline size.

Why: A small number of programs (e.g. Precision Production at +641%) would otherwise distort the mean and standard deviation, making thresholds meaningless for the rest of the portfolio.

How: Values outside [-100, 300] are replaced with the boundary value. Weighted mean and std are computed using 2019-2021 baseline completions as weights.

mu = 1.66 sigma = 20.62 High Risk z <= -2.0 net_pct <= -39.58
Method 02

Composite Three-Metric Risk Score

What: Three independent binary metrics are evaluated per program-credential combination. High Risk = 2 or more metrics flagged; Moderate = 1; Low = none.

Why: No single metric reliably identifies structural decline. A program with a bad five-year trend but strong recent recovery is different from one declining on all three dimensions simultaneously.

How: Metric 1 — net pct change (25th percentile cutoff = -15.6%). Metric 2 — 3+ consecutive YoY declines (p = 0.125 under null). Metric 3 — post-pandemic recovery 2022-2024.

25th pctile = -15.6% p(3 declines) = 0.125 Recovery threshold = -5%
Method 03

Pearson and Spearman Correlation

What: Pearson measures linear correlation between program net change and BLS occupational growth. Spearman measures rank-order correlation (robust to outliers).

Why: Running both together reveals whether any apparent relationship is driven by a few extreme programs (Pearson sensitive to outliers; Spearman is not).

How: Applied at CIP2 level first, then at CIP2 × AWLEVEL level. Significance tested at p < 0.05.

Pearson r = -0.12 p = 0.497 Spearman rho = 0.13 p = 0.469
Method 04

Cohen's d Effect Size

What: Standardised measure of the gap between program net percentage change and BLS occupational growth at the matching credential level. Classifies alignment strength.

Why: Correlation tells you direction; Cohen's d tells you the magnitude of the gap in standardised units. A field can be positively correlated but still have a large supply-demand gap.

How: d = |gap| / sqrt(mean(prog_var, bls_var)). Strong <0.2, Moderate 0.2-0.5, Weak 0.5-0.8, Misaligned >0.8.

Eng. Bach. gap = -17.7pp Pub. Admin gap = -22.4pp Library Sci. d = 0.08 (Strong)
Method 05

Weighted Least Squares (WLS) Residuals

What: Regresses program net change on BLS occupational growth, weighting by baseline program size. Programs whose actual change is far from the model prediction are flagged as anomalies.

Why: OLS would treat a 10-student program the same as a 400,000-student program. WLS gives large programs proportionally more weight, so mismatch flags are meaningful at scale.

How: Studentized residuals above ±1.25 flagged. R² = 0.007 confirms near-zero aggregate fit — the outliers are genuine anomalies, not noise around a trend.

R² = 0.007 Threshold = ±1.25 std Precision Prod. resid = 4.21
Method 06

Cross-Lagged Correlation

What: Tests whether this year's program completion trends predict next year's (or the year after's) employment trends, at lags of 0, 12, 24, and 36 months.

Why: If the aggregate correlation is near zero today, it could be because institutions respond with a delay rather than because they are not responding at all. The lag analysis distinguishes these two scenarios.

How: OEWS employment change and IPEDS YoY change are cross-correlated at each lag. Best lag selected by max |r|. Significance at p < 0.05.

Health: r=0.999 @ 24mo Education: r=-0.96 @ 12mo* Business: r=0.999 @ 24mo
Insights and Why They Make Sense
What the data reveals and the real-world logic behind each finding
📉

The aggregate signal is silent — on purpose Expected

Pearson r = -0.12 between national program trends and BLS demand sounds alarming but is actually informative. It means the supply-demand relationship cannot be read at the field level — you must look credential by credential. Aggregating across Associate, Bachelor's, and graduate completions within the same CIP field cancels out divergent signals, producing noise. This result motivates the entire credential-level disaggregation that follows.

⚠️

High-demand fields are shrinking at the Bachelor's level Critical

Engineering Bachelor's completions are down 8% while BLS projects +9.7% occupational growth — a 17.7 percentage point gap. Public Administration is down 16% against +6.2% demand. Health Professions and Business show similar patterns. This is not a demand problem; it is a supply problem driven by the enrollment cliff and pandemic-era attrition concentrated in programs with longer time-to-degree requirements.

🔴

Associate-level risk is structural, not cyclical High Risk

44 of 345 combinations carry Moderate or High sunset risk, concentrated at the Associate level in Liberal Arts, Library Sciences, Philosophy, Foreign Languages, and Communications Technology. These programs score High Risk on multiple metrics simultaneously — not just declining enrollment, but consecutive annual declines and failed post-pandemic recovery. That combination indicates structural disengagement rather than a temporary enrollment dip.

📈

Over-supply anomalies defy the national trend Anomaly

Precision Production grew 641% in completions against a -3.5% BLS demand outlook — a studentized residual of 4.21, the largest in the dataset. Construction Trades and Transportation also grew 330-350% against modest demand. These are likely driven by short-cycle certificate programs responding to regional demand signals not fully captured in national BLS projections, but they warrant monitoring for saturation risk.

⏱️

Institutions do respond — just slowly Responsive

Health Professions and Business both show near-perfect cross-lagged correlation (r = 0.999) at a 24-month lag. Education responds at 12 months (the only statistically significant result in the lag analysis, p = 0.041). This tells us that the system is not broken — it responds to labor market signals — but it operates on a 1-2 academic planning cycle delay. Public Administration is the exception, showing weak delayed response despite a large and growing demand gap.

🎓

The credential level is the key variable Core Finding

The same CIP field can show completely different risk profiles depending on credential level. Engineering at the Bachelor's level is misaligned (declining supply, growing demand). Engineering at the graduate level is stable. Library Sciences at the Associate level is Low Risk; at the graduate level it is High Risk. This means any policy response or program review that operates at the field level alone will draw the wrong conclusions. Credential-level disaggregation is not a refinement — it is the essential unit of analysis.

Phase 2 — Where This Goes Next
The same pipeline, extended across new dimensions using existing federal data sources

The infrastructure is already built. Phase 2 does not require new methodology — it applies the same pipeline to data that exists at finer levels of resolution. Each extension below is a natural second paper.

Extension 01

State-Level Disaggregation

Replace national OEWS files with state-specific OEWS data. Run the full pipeline for all 50 states, producing a CIP × credential alignment profile per state. A healthcare-heavy state and a manufacturing-heavy state will show very different risk signatures. This generates 50 built-in case studies from a single methodological extension.

Extension 02

Institution-Level Analysis

IPEDS unit-record data includes institution identifiers. Disaggregating to the institution level identifies which specific colleges are the primary drivers of national over- and under-supply patterns. This moves the work from descriptive to prescriptive and has direct application for accreditation review and state system planning.

Extension 03

Demographic Disaggregation

IPEDS completions data includes breakdowns by gender, race/ethnicity, and age group. Layering these dimensions in reveals whether labor market misalignment falls disproportionately on specific student populations — a dimension with significant equity relevance and high value for policy-focused journals.

Extension 04

Earnings Integration

Adding College Scorecard median earnings data by field and credential shifts the demand signal from occupational headcounts to actual graduate wage outcomes. This substantially strengthens the alignment scoring and enables a richer Phase 2 composite index combining BLS projections, employment rates, and earnings trajectories.
